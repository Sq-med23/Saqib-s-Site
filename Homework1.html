Writing response 2 "AI Slop"

From the article I just read, “With ‘AI slop’ distorting our reality, the world is walking into a big disaster with their eyes closed,” the author, Nesrine Malik, explains how artificial intelligence is beginning to blur the line between what is real and what is fake online. Reading this article made me agree even more with what the author is warning us about. AI has become so realistic that it is getting harder to tell the difference between authentic content and something completely generated. It is also unsettling to realize how much AI surrounds us every day without us even noticing. One of the main terms used in the article was “AI slop,” which refers to low-quality, computer-generated images or content that are constantly shown to us, often mixed in with real pictures of news, politics, and everyday life. This kind of content is designed to grab attention quickly and spread across the internet just as fast. In addition, it is not always harmless or meant for entertainment; in many cases, it involves political propaganda. For example, leaders like Donald Trump have appeared in completely made up videos meant to present one side as powerful and the other as weak. Due to this, I sometimes feel like I cannot fully trust what I see online anymore. It has reached a point where even hearing or seeing something makes you question where the information actually came from. If an elderly person were to come across something artificially generated, they could easily be misled or overwhelmed by it. This can lead to serious misunderstandings, not only for older generations but for people of all ages. I relate to the author because as AI becomes more common, everything starts to feel less genuine. Constantly having to double-check sources and research whether something is true adds stress and makes it harder to trust even basic information. Although there are moments when AI slop might seem harmless or even funny, giving people something to laugh at, in the long run it can slowly change expectations in unhealthy ways. When people discover that what they believed was real is actually fake, it can create confusion and frustration. I also understood the author’s point that being endlessly exposed to AI generated content can make even serious events, such as war or political violence, feel less real because they are mixed in with memes, trends, and entertainment. When I scroll through social media, I often see shocking news placed right next to jokes or aesthetic videos, and the contrast makes it difficult to process how I should react. AI generated videos sometimes numb the way you process or think about whether a video is true or not. It gives people a sense of reconsideration and has them question whether they just watched something real or fake. Overall, this article made me realize that the real danger may not just be AI itself, but how people consume the content from social media and end up confused, desensitized, and unsure of what to believe.
